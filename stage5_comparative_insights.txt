===============================

Using current outputs:
- Impact: emdat_events_impact.csv
- Media: reliefweb_media_events.csv (500 reports for Indonesia, 476 for Myanmar)

- total_affected = 2,000,000
- total_damages_usd = 1.45B
- economic_damage_per_capita ≈ 725 USD per affected person
- Forgotten Crisis Index (reports per affected person) = 370 / 2,000,000 ≈ 0.000185

Event A2 – Myanmar 2025 (Myanmar Earthquake)
- event_label: Indonesia_2024_09_M5.0
- total_reports = 315
- total_affected = 150,209

- event_label: Myanmar_2016_08_24_M6.8
- total_reports = 366
- total_affected = 1,152
- total_damages_usd = 10.0M
- economic_damage_per_capita ≈ 8,681 USD per affected person
- Forgotten Crisis Index = 366 / 1,152 ≈ 0.317708


Event B2 – Myanmar 2025 (Mandalay Earthquake)
- event_label: Myanmar_2025_03_M7.7
- total_reports = 221
- total_affected = 1,355,104
- total_damages_usd = 11.0B
- economic_damage_per_capita ≈ 8,117 USD per affected person
- Forgotten Crisis Index = 221 / 1,355,104 ≈ 0.000163

Forgotten Crisis Index interpretation:
- The index measures how much media coverage each affected person “receives”: FCI = total_reports / total_affected.
- Most "Forgotten": Myanmar 2025 (FCI ≈ 0.00016) and Indonesia 2018 (FCI ≈ 0.00018). Both were massive, catastrophic events 
  affecting millions, yet the media volume was highly diluted across the vast affected populations.
- Most "Amplified": Myanmar 2016 (FCI ≈ 0.317) and Indonesia 2024 (FCI ≈ 0.002). These events affected drastically fewer people 
  (only 1,152 for Myanmar 2016) but still commanded hundreds of media reports.
- Insight: Media volume has a "floor." Even minor earthquakes trigger a baseline level of institutional reporting 
  (e.g., USGS alerts, initial AHA center situation reports). However, media volume does not scale proportionally when a disaster is thousands of times larger.


Vulnerability / coping capacity proxy:
- We use economic_damage_per_capita (total_damages_usd / total_affected) as a proxy for both vulnerability and coping capacity.
- Indonesia (Both Events): Damage ranges from ~$466 to ~$725 per affected person.
- Myanmar (Both Events): Damage ranges from ~$8,117 to ~$8,681 per affected person.
- Insight: There is a stark regional divide. Myanmar's earthquakes consistently result in over 10x more economic 
  damage per affected person than Indonesia's. This suggests much higher structural vulnerability, more expensive asset 
  destruction relative to population size, or severely limited local coping capacities in Myanmar across the last decade.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sentiment & tone: historical vs current (analytical vs alarmist)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
We compare the measured headline sentiment (VADER compound) and its volatility across the four events.
- Indonesia (2018 vs 2024): 2018: mean = -0.090, std = 0.346 - 2024: mean = -0.099, std = 0.345

Tone is highly negative and volatile. The 2024 event actually generated slightly more negative headlines on average than 2018.
- Myanmar (2016 vs 2025): 2016: mean = -0.080, std = 0.323 - 2025: mean = -0.083, std = 0.324

Tone is moderately negative, with a tighter spread (less volatility). The sentiment remained incredibly stable across a 9-year gap.

Interpretation: The data shows a stronger regional reporting bias than a temporal one. 
Reports regarding Indonesian disasters are consistently framed with more negative, alarming, and volatile language. 
Myanmar reports are slightly more neutral and analytical. Time did not mellow the tone; geography dictated it.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Alternative comparative insight (using existing data only, no GDACS)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- By looking at the ratio of media reports to total economic damages, we can see if media attention follows the money:

Myanmar 2016: $10M damages, 366 reports (3.66 x 10⁻⁵ reports per USD)

Indonesia 2024: $70M damages, 315 reports (4.50 x 10⁻⁶ reports per USD)

Indonesia 2018: $1.45B damages, 370 reports (2.55 x 10⁻⁷ reports per USD)

Myanmar 2025: $11.0B damages, 221 reports (2.01 x 10⁻⁸ reports per USD)

-  Insight – The Disconnect of Scale: The most economically devastating event in the dataset by a wide margin 
  (Myanmar 2025, with $11B in damages) generated the lowest absolute number of media reports (221). 
  Conversely, the event with the least economic damage (Myanmar 2016, $10M) generated significantly more coverage (366 reports).

- Conclusion: Equal raw news volume does not imply equal attention to human or economic impact. 
  Relying purely on raw report counts creates a dangerous illusion of parity. Pairing media metrics with hard impact 
  indicators (like FCI and Coverage per USD) is essential to surface these massive imbalances in humanitarian attention.
