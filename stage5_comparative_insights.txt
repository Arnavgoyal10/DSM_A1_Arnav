Forgotten Crisis Index interpretation:
- The index measures how much media coverage each affected person “receives”: FCI = total_reports / total_affected.
- Most "Forgotten": Indonesia 2018 (FCI ≈ 0.00014) and Afghanistan 2023 (FCI ≈ 0.00084). Both were massive, catastrophic events 
  affecting hundreds of thousands to millions, yet the media volume was highly diluted across the vast affected populations.
- Most "Amplified": Afghanistan 2015 (FCI ≈ 0.0044) and Indonesia 2024 (FCI ≈ 0.00099). These events affected drastically fewer people 
  but still commanded relatively sizable chunks of media coverage.
- Insight: Media volume has a "floor." Even minor earthquakes trigger a baseline level of institutional reporting 
  (e.g., USGS alerts, initial situation reports). However, media volume does not scale proportionally when a disaster is ten times larger.


Vulnerability / coping capacity proxy:
- We use economic_damage_per_capita (total_damages_usd / total_affected) as a proxy for both vulnerability and coping capacity.
- Indonesia: Damage ranges from ~$466 (2024) to ~$725 (2018) per affected person.
- Afghanistan: Damage is ~$773 (2023) per affected person. (2015 omitted due to missing economic damage data).
- Insight: Economic damage per capita is remarkably similar across the recent catastrophic events for both nations, hovering around the $400 - $800 range. Despite Afghanistan's lower baseline GDP, the per-capita damage metric suggests profound structural vulnerability when massive earthquakes hit densely populated regions in both countries.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Sentiment & tone: historical vs current (analytical vs alarmist)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
We compare the measured headline sentiment (VADER compound) and its volatility across the four events.
- Indonesia (2018 vs 2024): 2018: mean = -0.113, std = 0.399 - 2024: mean = 0.042, std = 0.362

Tone was highly negative in 2018 (a massive catastrophe with over 4,000 deaths). By 2024 (a much smaller magnitude 5 event), the average sentiment flipped to slightly positive/neutral, showing a clear pivot from "alarmist panic" to "analytical capability/resilience."
- Afghanistan (2015 vs 2023): 2015: mean = -0.099, std = 0.356 - 2023: mean = -0.049, std = 0.358

Tone is moderately negative across both events. Crucially, the massive 2023 Herat earthquakes experienced less negative sentiment than the 2015 Hindu Kush quake, demonstrating a persistent "crisis fatigue" or normalized reporting tone even when faced with significant casualties.

Interpretation: 
Reports regarding Indonesian disasters scale their alarmism to the scale of the tragedy (huge jump in negativity for massive death tolls). 
Afghanistan reports remain persistently negative but stable over time, reflecting a normalized baseline of humanitarian crisis reporting where tone fluctuates much less regardless of the event's actual magnitude.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Alternative comparative insight (using existing data only, no GDACS)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- By looking at the ratio of media reports to total economic damages, we can see if media attention follows the money:

Indonesia 2024: $70M damages, 150 reports (2.14 x 10⁻⁶ reports per USD)

Afghanistan 2023: $217M damages, 238 reports (1.09 x 10⁻⁶ reports per USD)

Indonesia 2018: $1.45B damages, 293 reports (2.02 x 10⁻⁷ reports per USD)

-  Insight – The Disconnect of Scale: The most economically devastating event in the dataset by a wide margin 
  (Indonesia 2018, with $1.45B in damages) generated the lowest relative amount of coverage per dollar of damage. 
  The smallest event financially (Indonesia 2024) generated an order of magnitude more coverage per dollar.

- Conclusion: Equal raw news volume does not imply equal attention to human or economic impact. 
  Relying purely on raw report counts creates a dangerous illusion of parity. Pairing media metrics with hard impact 
  indicators (like FCI and Coverage per USD) is essential to surface these massive imbalances in humanitarian attention.
